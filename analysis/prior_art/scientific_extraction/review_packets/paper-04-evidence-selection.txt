## ABSTRACT_OBJECTIVE_AND_REPORTED_OUTCOME
Source range: L00005–L00018

L00005	Abstract.
L00006	Significance Melanin and hemoglobin have been measured as important diagnostic indicators of facial skin conditions for aesthetic and diagnostic purposes.
L00007	Commercial clinical equipment provides reliable analysis
L00008	results, but it has several drawbacks: exclusive to the acquisition system, expensive, and computationally intensive.
L00009	Aim We propose an approach to alleviate those drawbacks using a deep learning model trained to solve the forward problem of light–tissue interactions.
L00010	The model is structurally extensible for various light sources and cameras and maintains the input image resolution for medical applications.
L00011	Approach A facial image is divided into multiple patches and decomposed into melanin, hemoglobin, shading, and specular maps.
L00012	The outputs are reconstructed into a facial image by solving the forward problem over skin areas.
L00013	As learning progresses, the difference between the reconstructed image and input image is reduced, resulting in the melanin and hemoglobin maps becoming closer to their distribution of the input image.
L00014	Results The proposed approach was evaluated on 30
L00015	subjects using the professional clinical system, VISIA VAESTRO.
L00016	The correlation coefficients for melanin and hemoglobin were found to be 0.932 and 0.857, respectively.
L00017	Additionally, this approach was applied to simulated images with varying amounts of melanin and hemoglobin.
L00018	Conclusion The proposed approach showed high correlation with the clinical system for analyzing melanin and hemoglobin distribution, indicating its potential for accurate diagnosis.

## PROBLEM_CONTEXT_AND_STATED_CONTRIBUTION
Source range: L00023–L00046

L00023	Introduction The optical properties of human skin have been studied for various purposes.
L00024	Skin layers are optically modeled in computer vision for a detailed description of the face.
L00025	1 , 2 In dermatology, light absorption and scattering are the main principles for diagnosing skin conditions.
L00026	3 , 4 In biomedical research, the distribution of chromophores and hemodynamic changes are imaged using diffuse optical tomography, 5 optical coherence tomography, 6 and spatial frequency domain imaging.
L00027	7 In terms of skin analysis equipment, the optical measurement approach has various advantages compared to other imaging modalities: non-invasive, relatively safe, and real-time or frequent measurements.
L00028	As examples of single-measurement devices, the Mexameter, Sebumeter, and Visiometer can be used to quantify pigmentation, oiliness, and wrinkles, respectively.
L00029	8 , 9 Multifunctional systems utilize image processing techniques to skin images acquired from well-constructed optical systems with minimal ambient light.
L00030	For example, VISIA, Robo skin analyzer, and DermaVision provide the ability to measure melanin, hemoglobin, wrinkles, and pores.
L00031	10 – 12 Multifunctional systems analyze shape and color-related skin information based on the optical properties of human skin.
L00032	The shape features, such as the skin surface and facial structure, are enhanced in the specular light images, and the wrinkles 13 , 14 and pores 15 belong to these features.
L00033	Skin color is the result of the interactions between light and skin chromophores such as melanin and hemoglobin, 16 and this feature is enhanced in cross-polarized images.
L00034	17 For accurate measurements, clinical multi-functional systems are used to acquire and analyze skin images with ambient light blocked.
L00035	Despite the fact that these professional systems have produced reliable analysis
L00036	results and are widely used in clinics, they have several
L00037	limitations: the analytical tool is dedicated to the image acquisition system.
L00038	Images should be acquired in a specific environment and this result in a lack of versatility.
L00039	The image acquisition system and analysis tools require a high cost, and the analysis process is time-consuming due to the complex calculations involved, such as fitting for each pixel.
L00040	This is particularly important in medical applications that require high-resolution images, even if it needs high computational resources and time.
L00041	To alleviate those
L00042	limitations, we propose a novel approach using a deep learning model trained to solve the forward problem of light–tissue interactions.
L00043	The model is structurally extensible for various light sources and cameras and it maintains the resolution of the input image for medical applications.
L00044	The proposed approach is evaluated with the professional clinical system, VISIA VAESTRO.
L00045	In addition, this approach was applied to simulations with varying amounts of melanin and hemoglobin.

L00054	background, eyes, nostrils, and lips.
L00055	To extract the skin area from any face image, we developed a skin segmentation model using a lightened structure of the basic U-Net model with reduced number of channels.
L00056	The model was trained using 380 images with an input and output size of 640 × 480 pixels , achieving a performance of CIoU 0.95.
L00057	2.2 Patch Divider and Combiner When a high-resolution image is resized to the UNet input size, the detailed features of the image are lost.
L00058	This loss could be a critical problem in medical applications where diagnostics require detailed characteristics.
L00059	Therefore, the input image is divided into multiple patches to maintain the input image resolution.
L00060	To remove borderlines that may appear after merging, the patches are combined by multiplying the overlapping areas by the gradient weight, and the overlapping area is added to the patch size.
L00061	Since the image acquisition conditions for all patches are the same, the skin’s optical properties do not change even if analyzed in units of patches.
L00062	In this study, the input image is divided into 4 × 6 patches with a size of 256 × 256 pixels for inference.

## ILLUMINATION_CAMERA_AND_SKIN_OPTICS
Source range: L00078–L00120

L00078	This study primarily focuses on color-related skin components, melanin and hemoglobin maps.
L00079	As skin color is also affected by morphological factors, shading and specular maps are incorporated into the structure to account for their effects.
L00080	2.4 Forward Model 2.4.1 Image acquisition Figure 3 shows a schematic diagram of the skin image acquisition process, in which light emitted from the source is reflected from the skin, detected by a camera, and formed into an image.
L00081	The spectral characteristics of a light source, which cover all light that affects the image including ambient light, are expressed using spectral power distribution (SPD).
L00082	In a typical measurement environment, the light source is a combination of various indoor lights and sunlight.
L00083	In controlled conditions where ambient light is blocked, and images are acquired using professional equipment, the SPD of the light source of the equipment is supplied by the manufacturer’s specifications.
L00084	In uncontrolled conditions, where images are acquired in an open environment, images are affected by various light sources, and the SPD can be measured directly using a spectrometer.
L00085	Most of the light that illuminated the skin is diffused into the skin layers, and the rest is reflected from the surface.
L00086	In the skin layers, visible light is mainly absorbed by melanin and hemoglobin and scattered exponentially as a function of wavelength.
L00087	19 , 20 Skin reflectance is affected by various factors, including the scattering properties of the skin as well as the absorption of the melanin and hemoglobin, which have been extensively researched.
L00088	21 – 24 The specular light and diffusely reflected light are detected as photons by the camera sensor and then converted into electrical signals according to the quantum efficiency of the camera to form an image.
L00089	This image is in a RGB color space dependent on the camera device and is sequentially converted to the common color space XYZ, RGB, and sRGB.
L00090	25 Fig.
L00091	3 Schematic diagram of the skin image acquisition process.
L00092	2.4.2 Forward and inverse problem Under a given light and skin reflectance, the diffusely reflected light is predicted as a unique solution to a forward problem.
L00093	The radiative transfer equation (RTE), diffuse equation, 26 or a stochastic
L00094	methods Monte Carlo simulation 27 , 28 can be used to solve this mathematical model.
L00095	Acquiring skin images and analyzing skin components is the opposite process.
L00096	It is difficult to obtain the actual values of skin properties because skin is a scattering-dominant material.
L00097	Therefore, it has been predicted by solving the inverse problem using Inverse Monte Carlo, 29 , 30 principal component analysis (PCA), 31 independent component analysis (ICA), 32 or polynomial fitting.
L00098	33 During the fitting process, different skin property values are iteratively given, and the corresponding forward problems are solved until the error becomes lower than the threshold.
L00099	It requires enough time to repeatedly calculate the forward problem for each pixel of the whole image.
L00100	As the forward problem has been studied for a long time and applied to various studies, 34 , 35 it is considered reliable and used to train the proposed model.
L00101	2.4.3 Acquisition conditions The image acquisition environment must be set in advance to proceed with the forward model.
L00102	There are two factors: a light source and camera sensitivity.
L00103	In this study, skin images were acquired using the “VISIA-CR” system.
L00104	To ensure consistency between image acquisition and analysis conditions, the spectral data of the standard illuminant “D65” in ISO 2002 and the camera sensitivity of the “Canon 5D Mark II” 36 , 37 within the wavelength range of 400 to 720 nm are used.
L00105	The spectrum of the light source is normalized by inversely multiplying a scaling factor, which is derived by multiplying the light source and the maximum sensitivity value of the camera channels.
L00106	2.4.4 Optical modeling of skin layers The epidermis is the outermost layer of the skin.
L00107	In the epidermis layer, light is absorbed by melanin and scattered by keratin fibers.
L00108	However, the change in light direction could be ignored because of the thin thickness.
L00109	In the dermis layer, light mainly experiences absorption in the hemoglobin of the capillaries and blood vessels, and scattering in collagen fibers and elastin fibers.
L00110	38 Therefore, skin layers are optically modeled as follows: the epidermis is modeled as a melanin absorption layer, and the dermis is modeled as a combination of a hemoglobin absorption layer and multiple scattering layers.
L00111	The measured light includes both the specular reflected light from the skin surface and the diffusely reflected light that is absorbed in the epidermis and dermis layers.
L00112	2.4.5 Skin reflectance The Kubelka–Munk theory is one of the popular
L00113	methods for modeling light reflection in skin layers.

L00115	the fraction of light that transmitted the epidermis, and R dermis refers to the fraction of light reflected from the dermis layer.
