L00016	Abstract: Artificial intelligence (AI) in dermatology has moved beyond the early paradigm of single-image
L00017	classification. Dermatological diagnosis is achieved based on morphology, distribution, symptoms, tactile findings,
L00018	temporal evolution, patient history, histopathology, and treatment response. Clinically important differentials, such as
L00019	eczema versus psoriasis, cutaneous T-cell lymphoma versus chronic dermatitis, drug eruption versus viral exanthem,
L00020	lupus erythematosus versus dermatomyositis, and melanoma versus atypical nevus, are rarely resolved with one
L00021	photograph alone. This review therefore frames AI-assisted dermatology around a central argument: the field must
L00022	progress from lesion recognition to dermatology-specific multimodal clinical reasoning. We summarize major advances
L00023	in convolutional neural networks, dermoscopic benchmarks, clinical-image datasets, large language models, vision-
L00024	language systems, and dermatology foundation models. We also analyze challenges that are particularly relevant
L00025	to dermatology, including morphologic overlap, skin-tone bias, reduced erythema visibility on darker skin, dataset
L00026	imbalance, variable smartphone imaging, imperfect reference standards, and the gap between benchmark performance
L00027	and clinical deployment. Special attention is given to fairness, regulatory oversight, software as a medical device,
L00028	human-AI collaboration, prognosis prediction, biologic-response modeling, longitudinal monitoring, and treatment
L00029	optimization. Finally, we discuss future directions, including skin-tone-aware foundation models, lesion-level and
L00030	body-site grounding, pathology-genomics integration, dermatology copilots, post-marketing surveillance, and
L00031	prospective clinical trials. By prioritizing dermatological reasoning rather than generic AI architecture, this review
L00032	outlines a clinically grounded pathway for building safe, interpretable, equitable, and useful AI systems for skin disease
L00033	management.
