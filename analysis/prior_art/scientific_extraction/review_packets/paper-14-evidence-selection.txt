## BACKGROUND_AND_STUDY_OBJECTIVE
Source range: L00020–L00028

L00020	Introduction
L00021	Background In dermatology, artificial intelligence (AI) is poised to improve the efficiency and accuracy of traditional diagnostic approaches, including visual examination, skin biopsy, and histopathologic examination [ 1 ].
L00022	Deep-learning image recognition models have had success in differentiating between dermatological diseases using images of light-skinned individuals.
L00023	However, when these models are tested on images of people with skin of color, the performance drops [ 2 ].
L00024	It is thought that the primary reason for this difference is the lack of available images of dermatological diseases in individuals with darker skin color (Fitzpatrick classification of skin types 4 and 5) [ 3 ].
L00025	However, is it also possible that even when the same number of images are available, image recognition models will have a harder time differentiating between dermatological diseases in individuals with Fitzpatrick skin types 4 and 5 compared to skin types 1, 2, and 3?
L00026	Objective The
L00027	objective of this research was to assess whether image recognition models perform differently when differentiating between dermatological diseases in individuals of color (Fitzpatrick skin types 4 and 5) than when differentiating between the same dermatological diseases in Caucasians (Fitzpatrick skin types 1, 2, and 3)
L00028	when both models are trained on an equal number of images.

## DATA_SOURCES_MODEL_AND_TRAINING
Source range: L00029–L00046

L00029	Methods Open-source images of melanoma and basal cell carcinoma (BCC) were acquired from the Hellenic Dermatological Atlas [ 4 ], the Dermatology Atlas [ 5 ], the Interactive Dermatology Atlas [ 6 ], and DermNet NZ [ 7 ].
L00030	Two image recognition models were trained, validated, and tested using
L00031	methodology as described previously [ 8 ].
L00032	TensorFlow [ 9 ], an open-source software library by Google, was used as a deep-learning framework and was used to retrain Inception, version 3 (v3).
L00033	Inception v3 is a deep convolutional neural network.
L00034	This neural network consists of a hierarchy of multiple computational layers that each have an input and output.
L00035	All layers except the final layer of this neural network are pretrained with more than 1.2 million images.
L00036	The final layer of the neural network was retrained with the gathered dermatological images.
L00037	During the retraining process, the neural network underwent both a training and validation step.
L00038	In the training step, the inputted images were used to train the neural network.
L00039	In the validation step, inputted naïve images were used to iteratively assess training accuracy [ 10 ].
L00040	After the model had been retrained (trained and validated), a user-inputted testing/assessment step was performed in which test images were inputted and the
L00041	results were statistically analyzed.
L00042	The program assessment output is expressed in terms of percentages of the probability of each of the dermatological manifestations for each testing image inputted.
L00043	R software (R Foundation for Statistical Computing) [ 11 ] was used to perform the statistical analysis.
L00044	Sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value (PPV), negative predictive value (NPV), and F1 score were calculated for each dermatological manifestation.
L00045	The F1 score is the harmonic average of the sensitivity and PPV (mean of the recall and precision).
L00046	The goal of each model was to differentiate between melanoma and BCC.

L00051	Area under the receiver operating characteristic (AUC) curves for melanoma and BCC were calculated to determine the performance of the two models.

## PERFORMANCE_RESULTS
Source range: L00052–L00063

L00052	Results When asked to differentiate between melanoma and BCC, the image recognition model trained and validated on images of light skin color had higher sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, and F1 score than the image recognition model trained and validated on images of skin of color
L00053	( Table 1 ).
L00054	In predicting melanoma, the image recognition model trained and validated on images of light skin color had a sensitivity of 0.60, specificity of 0.53, PPV of 0.56, NPV of 0.57, and F1 score of 0.58.
L00055	On the other hand, in predicting melanoma, the same image recognition model trained and validated on images of skin of color had a sensitivity of 0.53, specificity of 0.47, PPV of 0.50, NPV of 0.50, and F1 score of 0.52.
L00056	In predicting BCC, the image recognition model trained and validated on images of light skin color had a sensitivity of 0.53, specificity of 0.60, PPV of 0.57, NPV of 0.56, and F1 score of 0.55.
L00057	On the other hand, for prediction of BCC, the same image recognition model trained and validated on images of skin of color had a sensitivity of 0.47, specificity of 0.53, PPV of 0.50, NPV of 0.50, and F1 score of 0.48.
L00058	The average AUC for the two light skin color image recognition models was 0.598, compared to 0.500 (values point out the difference) for the skin of color image recognition models ( Table 1 and Figure 1 ).

## LIMITATIONS_AND_CONCLUSION
Source range: L00064–L00071

L00064	Discussion
L00065	Limitations The number of images available was limited for Fitzpatrick skin types 4 and 5; as such, both the light skin color and skin of color models were investigated with this constraint for the number of images used during training.
L00066	A larger sample size would have been better to test if the
L00067	results recur consistently.
L00068	Conclusion When the same number of images is used for training, validation, and testing, the AI model that was provided images of melanoma and BCC belonging to Fitzpatrick classification skin types 1, 2, and 3 performed better than the AI model that was provided with
L00069	images of melanoma and BCC in skin types 4 and 5.
L00070	This may be because dermatological diseases can have more variability in presentation in individuals with darker skin; additionally, cutaneous manifestations may not be as easily distinguished from the surrounding skin in darker-skinned individuals.
L00071	As such, a higher number of images of skin of color with dermatological diseases than images of light skin color with dermatological diseases would need to be gathered for the AI models to perform equally well.
