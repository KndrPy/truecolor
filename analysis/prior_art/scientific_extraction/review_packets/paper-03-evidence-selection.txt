## ABSTRACT_AND_STATED_CONTRIBUTION
Source range: L00010–L00019

L00010	The remarkable progress of deep learning in dermatological tasks has brought us closer to achieving diagnostic accuracies comparable to those of human experts.
L00011	However, while large
L00012	datasets play a crucial role in the development of reliable deep neural network models, the quality of data therein and their correct usage are of paramount importance.
L00013	Several factors can impact data quality, such as the presence of duplicates, data leakage across train-test partitions, mislabeled images, and the absence of a well-defined test partition.
L00014	In this paper, we conduct meticulous analyses of three popular dermatological image
L00015	datasets: DermaMNIST, its source HAM10000, and Fitzpatrick17k, uncovering these data quality issues, measure the effects of these problems on the benchmark
L00016	results, and propose corrections to the
L00017	datasets.
L00018	Besides ensuring the reproducibility of our analysis, by making our analysis pipeline and the accompanying code publicly available, we aim to encourage similar explorations and to facilitate the identification and addressing of potential data quality issues in other large
L00019	datasets.

## DERMAMNIST_SOURCE_AND_RESOLUTION
Source range: L00086–L00105

L00086	Results DermaMNIST Released as a biomedical imaging
L00087	dataset equivalent to the MNIST
L00088	dataset of handwritten digits, MedMNIST consists of images from standardized biomedical imaging
L00089	datasets resized to MNIST-like 28 × 28 resolution.
L00090	Despite being a fairly new
L00091	dataset, it has been quite popular (1009 citations as of November, 2024: 633 citations of [a more recent] 2023 paper 39 , and 376 of [an older] 2021 version of the paper 38 ).
L00092	The dermatological subset of MedMNIST, DermaMNIST, contains resized images from the popular “Human Against Machine with 10000 training images” (HAM10000)
L00093	dataset 15 .
L00094	HAM10000 contains 10,015 dermoscopic images of pigmented skin lesions collected from patients at two study sites in Australia and Austria, with their diagnoses confirmed by either histopathology, confocal microscopy, clinical follow-up visits, or expert consensus.
L00095	The 7 disease labels in the
L00096	dataset cover 95% of the lesions encountered in clinical practice 15 .
L00097	Because of these meritorious properties, HAM10000 is a good candidate
L00098	dataset for dermatological analysis, as aimed for with DermaMNIST.
L00099	While the “ lightweight ” nature of DermaMNIST due to its “ small size ” is appealing for its adoption in machine learning for biomedical imaging 39 , the low spatial resolution (28 × 28) does not capture sufficient morphological structures of skin lesions compared
L00100	to the source
L00101	dataset HAM10000.
L00102	Despite this, DermaMNIST has been used for a wide variety of applications in peer-reviewed publications: semi- and self-supervised learning 41 – 43 , federated learning 44 – 46 , privacy-preserving learning 47 , 48 , neural architecture search 49 , 50 , adversarially robust learning
L00103	51 , 52 , data augmentation 53 , 54 , generative modeling 55 , model interpretability 56 , AutoML 57 , active learning 58 , quantum vision transformers 59 , and biomedical vision-language foundation models 60 – 62 , as well as derivative benchmark
L00104	datasets 63 , 64 .
L00105	However, as we investigate below, the resulting DermaMNIST and its benchmarks suffer from serious flaws.

## DERMAMNIST_PARTITION_LEAKAGE
Source range: L00106–L00128

L00106	Data Leakage A caveat of HAM10000, despite its rather large size, is that it contains multiple images of the same lesion captured either from different viewing angles or at different magnification levels (Fig.
L00107	1(a) ), i.e., the number of lesions with unique lesion IDs (HAM_xxx) is smaller than the number of images with unique image IDs (ISIC_xxx).
L00108	We visualize the frequency counts of lesions and how many images of the same lesion are present in HAM10000 in Fig.
L00109	1(b) and observe that the 10,015 images are in fact derived from only 7,470 unique lesions, and 1,956 of these lesion IDs (~26.18%) contains 2 or more images: 1,423 lesions have 2 images, 490 lesions have 3 images, 34 lesions have 4 images, 5 lesions
L00110	have 5 images, and 6 lesions have 4 images each.
L00111	Unfortunately, this was not accounted for when preparing train-valid-test splits for DermaMNIST.
L00112	The DermaMNIST
L00113	dataset is released as a pre-processed NumPy array, so while the
L00114	dataset does not contain filenames of the images, we confirmed this by contacting the authors to obtain the exact training-validation-testing split filenames.
L00115	Therefore, there is considerable data leakage of images of the same lesion across partitions.
L00116	Fig.
L00117	1(a) shows 2 examples where images of the same lesion are present in the training, validation, and testing partitions.
L00118	This issue is quite pervasive across DermaMNIST, and our analysis found the following overlaps across partitions: train-test: 886 images (641 lesions), train-valid: 440 images (332 lesions); valid-test: 128 images (113 lesions); train-valid-test: 51 images (40 lesions) (Fig.
L00119	1(c) ).
L00120	Such data leakage naturally raises concerns on the reliability of the DermaMNIST benchmarks and related studies.
L00121	Fig.
L00122	1 DermaMNIST analyses: (a,b) show instances of and reasons for the data leakage, and (c) visualizes how the three
L00123	datasets: DermaMNIST, DermaMNIST-C, and DermaMNIST-E differ in their partition composition, yet have similarly proportionate diagnosis distributions.
L00124	Images from DermaMNIST are licensed under CC BY-NC 4.0 38 , 39 .
L00125	Best viewed online.
L00126	We correct this data leakage by simply moving all images of a lesion ID present in the train partition from valid and test partitions back to the train partition.
L00127	We choose to do this (i.e., moving the images of a lesion to the training set) instead of removing the images completely, to ensure that images of the same lesion are in one partition while also not discarding any images.
L00128	Although this has the undesirable side effect of increasing the training partition size at the cost of reduced validation and testing partition sizes, it fixes the data leakage issue by ensuring there is no overlap across partitions.

L00403	DermaMNIST Data leakage and benchmarks The extent of data leakage in DermaMNIST emanating from improper data partitioning is quite severe, with 1,006 of the 7,470 unique lesions (~13.47%) in the
L00404	dataset being present in more than 1 partition (Fig.
L00405	1(c) ).
L00406	Patient-level stratification, or in this case, lesion-level stratification, is crucial to ensure that the model does not “see” the lesions in the held-out test set while training.
L00407	Consequently, the lack of such a stratification implies that the corresponding benchmark classification
L00408	results are not truly reflective of the models’ generalization capability and, therefore, we strongly suspect that the models’ performance could be inflated.
L00409	On the other hand, since most works use the entire HAM10000
L00410	dataset for training instead of splitting it like in the case of DermaMNIST, undetected duplicates in HAM10000 are arguably a less severe issue.
L00411	However, a word of caution on comparing the benchmark
L00412	results of DermaMNIST and DermaMNIST-C (Table 1 ): the two
L00413	datasets do not share the same test partitions.
L00414	Specifically, DermaMNIST-C’s test set was obtained from DermaMNIST’s test set by removing all images whose lesion IDs were present in the test set, and therefore, the former is a subset of the other (i.e., DermaMNIST-C test set ⊂ DermaMNIST test set).
L00415	Similarly, as shown in Fig.
L00416	1(c) , the training set of DermaMNIST-C is larger than that of DermaMNIST.
L00417	For these reasons, contrary to the assumption that fixing data leakage in DermaMNIST should decrease performance benchmarks, we emphasize that performance benchmarks for DermaMNIST and DermaMNIST-C should not be compared since these models have been trained and evaluated on dissimilar partitions.
L00418	A more challenging
L00419	dataset While HAM10000 is indeed a valuable
L00420	dataset for skin image analysis research, models trained on HAM10000 do not necessarily perform well when evaluated on other skin lesion image

L00433	resolution images (28 × 28).
L00434	However, this is not the case with DermaMNIST
L00435	results, where ResNet-18/28 × 28 models perform [AUC; ACC] better than ResNet-50/224 × 224 models: [0.917; 0.735] versus [0.912; 0.731].
L00436	On the other hand, with DermaMNIST-C and DermaMNIST-E, ResNet-50/224 × 224 models do perform better than their ResNet-18/28 × 28 counterparts.

L00438	dataset (16,577 images), manual review of images to verify duplicates is virtually impossible due to the huge combinatorial space: there are \documentclass[12pt]{minimal} \usepackage{amsmath} \usepackage{wasysym} \usepackage{amsfonts} \usepackage{amssymb} \usepackage{amsbsy} \usepackage{mathrsfs} \usepackage{upgreek} \setlength{\oddsidemargin}{-69pt} \begin{document}$$\left(\begin{array}{c}16,577\\ 2\end{array}\right)=137$$\end{document} 16 , 577 2 = 137 million pairs of images.
L00439	Even worse, if we want to verify triplicates (i.e., 3 images that are copies of one another), we would have to review \documentclass[12pt]{minimal} \usepackage{amsmath} \usepackage{wasysym} \usepackage{amsfonts} \usepackage{amssymb} \usepackage{amsbsy} \usepackage{mathrsfs} \usepackage{upgreek} \setlength{\oddsidemargin}{-69pt} \begin{document}$$\left(\begin{array}{c}16,577\\ 3\end{array}\right)=759$$\end{document} 16 , 577 3 = 759 billion clusters of 3 images, and

L00443	results confirmed a near-perfect agreement with the algorithm’s
L00444	results.
L00445	Our experiments showed that using a second method of duplicate detection (cleanvision) was able to discover an additional 19 pairs of duplicates.
L00446	These pairs were also detected by fastdup, but their similarity scores fell just short of the chosen thresholds of 0.90 and 0.95, and therefore were absent from our manual review of fastdup’s
L00447	results.
L00448	Our
L00449	dataset cleaning pipeline, publicly available on GitHub 105 , is highly modular and configurable, allowing users to adjust similarity thresholds to exclude and use multiple duplicate lists (fastdup and cleanvision) for cleaning, decide whether they want to remove duplicate clusters altogether or retain one representative
L00450	image from each cluster, decide whether they want to remove images with unknown FST labels, and decide which outliers to exclude based on a similarity threshold.
L00451	While we can claim with a high degree of certainty that the new cleaned
L00452	dataset Fitzpatrick17k-C will be devoid of any duplicates, the large scale of manual review required makes it nearly impossible to guarantee it.
L00453	Dealing with conflicting diagnosis and FST labels Several duplicates in Fitzpatrick17k, despite being near identical copies, do not share the same diagnosis labels.
L00454	This affects the training and evaluation of models, since a model can be incorrectly penalized for its prediction because of the conflicting labels.
L00455	However, correcting these labels, so that copies of the same image have the same diagnosis label, requires a domain expert (i.e., a dermatologist) to go through all the images and confirm and correct their labels.
L00456	Unfortunately, even if such an endeavor were to be undertaken, these diagnoses will not be histopathology-confirmed and the accuracy of diagnoses confirmed through images alone (a scenario similar to “store-and-forward” teledermatology) is expected to be lower than those confirmed through in-person patient visits 106 –
L00457	108 .
L00458	Another approach to resolving the diagnosis label conflicts could be through mapping the diagnoses in Fitzpatrick17k to the World Health Organization (WHO) International Classification of Diseases, Eleventh Revision (ICD-11) 109 , and if multiple diagnoses belong to the same “parent”, their label conflict can be
L00459	resolved by assigning the “parent”’s label to both.
L00460	However, when mapping the diagnoses in Fitzpatrick17k to using the ICD-11 Browser 110 we ran into the following issues: Several diagnosis labels did not yield any matches (e.g., “acquired autoimmune bullous diseaseherpes gestationis”, “nematode infection”, “neurotic excoriations”, “pediculosis lids”).
L00461	There were diagnosis labels where one of the labels has an entry in ICD-11, but other seemingly related labels do not (e.g., “basal cell carcinoma” exists in ICD-11, but possibly related “basal cell carcinoma morpheiform” and “solid cystic basal cell carcinoma” do not).
L00462	There were labels where we found near but not exact matches (e.g., the label “erythema annulare centrifigum” in Fitzpatrick17k does not have an exact match, but ICD-11 contains “Erythema annulare”; other partial matching [Fitzpatrick17k; ICD-11] labels include ["hidradenitis”; “Hidradenitis suppurativa”], ["fixed eruptions”, “Fixed drug eruption”],
L00463	["lupus subacute”, “Subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus”], ["porokeratosis actinic”, “Disseminated superficial porokeratosis actinic”], etc.
L00464	Overall, 8 diagnosis labels had no matches in ICD-11.
L00465	An additional 29 diagnoses yielded partial matches to entries in ICD-11, and therefore could not be reliably mapped to a single entry.
L00466	For these diagnoses, i.e., those that had partial or no matches in ICD-11, we expanded our search to the ICD-11 Coding Tool Mortality and Morbidity Statistics (MMS) 111 and the ICD-11 Classification of Dermatological Diseases 112 , 113 , but this did not resolve any
L00467	issues.
L00468	Additionally, we also carried out a similar diagnosis lookup on the Systematized Medical Nomenclature of Medicine - Clinical Terminology (SNOMED-CT), a comprehensive clinical global healthcare terminology that may better represent medical vocabulary than ICD-11 114 .
L00469	Although we were able to find more matches to SNOMED-CT Identifiers 115 (SCTIDs) than to entries in ICD-11 and its derivatives, it was still not perfect: we ended up with 17 and 3 diagnosis labels with partial and no matches, respectively.
L00470	We make the
L00471	results of our Fitzpatrick17k diagnosis-to-{ICD-11 classification code, SCTID} mapping publicly available online.
L00472	As a recommendation for the future
L00473	datasets containing clinical images of skin diseases, we believe that adding either ICD classification codes or SNOMED-CT Identifiers (SCTIDs) for the disease labels would be a helpful addition to the metadata, and would greatly enhance the usability of such
L00474	datasets for hierarchical diagnosis
L00475	methods 7 – 9 .
L00476	Similar conflicts were observed for FST labels of images, where images with very high similarity varied in their FST labels, sometimes by as much as 4 tones (Fig.
L00477	9 ).
L00478	A potential solution to this would be to re-assess the images with conflicting FST labels.
L00479	This would involve either obtaining manual and verified healthy skin segmentation masks or using an automated healthy skin segmentation method, followed by mapping the skin tone estimated using these healthy skin pixels to an FST label.
L00480	However, as noted by Groh et al .
L00481	20 , collecting manual segmentation masks is expensive, and automated skin segmentation approaches suffer from their own set of challenges 116 , 117 , including but not limited to susceptibility to non-standardized illumination, the presence of multiple objects, and/or low quality images (we direct the
L00482	readers to our arXiv pre-print 100 which contains several more visualizations which could not make it to the publisher’s version of the paper).
L00483	The presence of diffuse pathologies such as “acne”, “acne vulgaris”, and “disseminated actinic porokeratosis”, where the diseased regions of the skin are spread over a wide region and the exact boundaries between diseased and healthy regions are not well-defined, further compounds the task for any
L00484	automated healthy skin detection approach.
L00485	Because of these intractable issues, we make the decision of removing duplicate images that have conflicting diagnosis or FST labels.

L00550	datasets: DermaMNIST from the MedMNIST collection, its source HAM10000, and Fitzpatrick17k.
L00551	For DermaMNIST, we investigated the extent of data leakage across its partitions and proposed two new and improved versions: a corrected
L00552	dataset that does not have any leakage (DermaMNIST-C) and an extended and arguably more challenging
L00553	dataset (DermaMNIST-E) that is almost the same as the ISIC 2018 Challenge
L00554	dataset except the images are resized to 28 × 28 and 224 × 224 and the “easter egg” image is removed from the test partition.
L00555	We also investigated the presence of duplicates in HAM10000, the source
L00556	dataset for DermaMNIST, and discovered 18 new duplicate image pairs that were unaccounted for in the metadata.
L00557	For Fitzpatrick17k, we conducted a systematic analysis encompassing data duplication, mislabeling of diagnosis and skin tone labels, as well as the identification of outlier images, followed by cleaning the
L00558	dataset to propose a cleaned version Fitzpatrick17k-Cwith standardized training, validation, and testing partitions.
L00559	We also showed how Fitzpatrick17k contains labels that do not fully align, which we recommend they should, with internationally recognized standards such as ICD-11 and SNOMED-CT, limiting its utility for hierarchical and differential diagnosis-based approaches.
L00560	For all the
L00561	datasets, we conducted benchmark evaluations using multiple
L00562	methods, repeated 3 times for robustness.
L00563	The primary
L00564	objective of this paper is to raise awareness about potential data quality issues that may arise in large
L00565	datasets, and how these issues can go unnoticed even in popular
L00566	datasets, casting doubts on the
L00567	conclusions made about the robustness and the generalizability of the models trained on these
L00568	datasets.

L00571	datasets.
