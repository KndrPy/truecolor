L00010	The remarkable progress of deep learning in dermatological tasks has brought us closer to achieving diagnostic accuracies comparable to those of human experts.
L00011	However, while large
L00012	datasets play a crucial role in the development of reliable deep neural network models, the quality of data therein and their correct usage are of paramount importance.
L00013	Several factors can impact data quality, such as the presence of duplicates, data leakage across train-test partitions, mislabeled images, and the absence of a well-defined test partition.
L00014	In this paper, we conduct meticulous analyses of three popular dermatological image
L00015	datasets: DermaMNIST, its source HAM10000, and Fitzpatrick17k, uncovering these data quality issues, measure the effects of these problems on the benchmark
L00016	results, and propose corrections to the
L00017	datasets.
L00018	Besides ensuring the reproducibility of our analysis, by making our analysis pipeline and the accompanying code publicly available, we aim to encourage similar explorations and to facilitate the identification and addressing of potential data quality issues in other large
L00019	datasets.

L00022	Introduction Skin diseases are the most common reason for clinical consultations in studied populations 1 , affecting almost a third of the global population 2 , 3 .
L00023	The 2013 Global Burden of Disease found skin diseases to be the fourth leading cause of nonfatal disabilities globally, accounting for 41.6 million Disability Adjusted Life Years and 39.0 million Years Lost due to Disability 4 .
L00024	In the USA alone, the healthcare cost of skin diseases was estimated to be $75 billion in 2016 5 .
L00025	Of all the skin diseases, skin cancer is a particularly concerning skin disease condition that merits special attention due to its potential seriousness.
L00026	With the increased incidence rates of skin cancer over the past decades 6 , coupled with the projected decline in the ratio of dermatologists to populations 5 , automated systems for dermatological diagnosis can be immensely valuable.
L00027	Advances in deep learning (DL)-based
L00028	methods for dermatological tasks have produced models that are approaching the diagnostic accuracies of experts, some even mimicking clinical approaches of hierarchical 7 – 9 and differential 10 diagnoses.
L00029	The data-driven nature of these DL
L00030	methods implies that large and diverse
L00031	datasets are needed to train accurate, robust, and generalizable models.
L00032	However, unlike natural computer vision
L00033	datasets, medical image
L00034	datasets are relatively smaller, primarily because of the large costs associated with image acquisition and annotation, legal, ethical, and privacy concerns 11 , and are more cost prohibitive to expand 12 .
L00035	This is also true for skin cancer image
L00036	datasets 13 , 14 , where the surge in skin image analysis research over the past decade can be attributed in part to recent publicly available
L00037	datasets, most notably the
L00038	datasets and challenges of the International Skin Imaging Collaboration (ISIC) and the associated HAM10000 15 and BCN2000 16
L00039	datasets, which are primarily dermoscopic image
L00040	datasets, as well as other clinical image
L00041	datasets such as SD-198 17 , SD-260 18 , derm7pt 19 , and Fitzpatrick17k 20 .
L00042	Although large data sets are important for the development of reliable models, the quality of the data therein and their correct use are equally important 21 – 24 : low-quality data may result in inefficient training, inaccurate models that exhibit biases, poor generalizability and low
L00043	robustness, and may negatively affect the interpretability of such models.
L00044	The data quality can be affected by several factors: mislabeled images, data leakage across training and evaluation partitions, the absence of a held-out test partition, etc.
L00045	The issue of data leakage, in particular, is in fact quite widespread, and a recent survey 25 of 17 fields spanning 294 articles, on topics ranging from medicine and bioinformatics to information technology operations and computer security, showed that ML adoption in all these fields
L00046	suffers from data leakage.
L00047	In an analysis of 10 popular natural computer vision, natural language, and audio
L00048	datasets, Northcutt et al .
L00049	26 estimated an average label error rate of at least 3.3%.
L00050	In medical image analysis domains, too, investigation into the use of machine learning best practices has found several instances of incorrect data partitioning and feature leakage between training and evaluation partitions.
L00051	Oner et al .
L00052	27 showed that a peer-reviewed article published in Nature Medicine using DL for histopathology image analysis suffered from data leakage by using slide-level stratification for data partitioning instead of patient-level stratification.
L00053	In a large-scale study, Bussola et al .
L00054	28 showed how DL models can exhibit considerably inflated performance measures when evaluated on
L00055	datasets where histopathology image patches from the same subject are present in training and validation partitions.
L00056	In mammography analysis, Samala et al .
L00057	29 , 30 showed the risks of feature leakage between training and validation partitions and how this could lead to an overly optimistic performance on the validation partition, compared to a completely held out test partition.
L00058	Similar investigations have been conducted on the adverse effects of incorrect data partitioning on test performance in optical coherence tomography (OCT) image classification 31 , brain magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) classification 32 , and longitudinal brain MRI analysis 33 Specific to skin image analysis, our
L00059	previous work 34 showed that the popular ISIC Skin Lesion Segmentation Challenge
L00060	datasets from 2016 through 2018 have considerable overlap among their training partitions, and that 706 images are present in all three
L00061	datasets’ training splits, a surprising discovery since ISIC 2016 only has 900 training images.
L00062	Cassidy et al .
L00063	35 analyzed the ISIC Skin Lesion Diagnosis Challenge
L00064	datasets from 2016 to 2020, and found overlap and duplicates across these
L00065	datasets.
L00066	They used a duplicate removal strategy to curate new clean training, validation, and testing sets.
L00067	Vega et al .
L00068	36 found that a popular monkeypox skin image
L00069	dataset, used in several peer-reviewed publications, contained “medically irrelevant images” and that models trained on these images did not necessarily rely on features underlying the diseases.
L00070	Very recently, Groger et al .
L00071	37 carried out an analysis of six dermatology skin data sets (MED-NODE, PH2, DDI, derm7pt, PAD-UFES-20, and SD-128), detecting and removing near duplicates and “irrelevant samples” from them.
L00072	However, all the
L00073	datasets in their study were small, with 3 of 6
L00074	datasets (MED-NODE, PH2, DDI) consisting of less than 700 images and the largest (SD-128) containing 5,619 images.
L00075	In this paper, we look at three popular and large skin image analysis
L00076	datasets: the DermaMNIST
L00077	dataset 38 , 39 and its source HAM10000
L00078	dataset 15 (10,015 images) and the Fitzpatrick17k
L00079	dataset 20 (16,577 images).
L00080	We perform systematic analyses of both
L00081	datasets and report instances of data duplication, data leakage across training and evaluation partitions, and data mislabeling in both
L00082	datasets, fixing them where possible.
L00083	Our corrected
L00084	datasets and detailed analysis
L00085	results are available online on Zenodo 40 .
L00086	Results DermaMNIST Released as a biomedical imaging
L00087	dataset equivalent to the MNIST
L00088	dataset of handwritten digits, MedMNIST consists of images from standardized biomedical imaging
L00089	datasets resized to MNIST-like 28 × 28 resolution.
L00090	Despite being a fairly new
L00091	dataset, it has been quite popular (1009 citations as of November, 2024: 633 citations of [a more recent] 2023 paper 39 , and 376 of [an older] 2021 version of the paper 38 ).
L00092	The dermatological subset of MedMNIST, DermaMNIST, contains resized images from the popular “Human Against Machine with 10000 training images” (HAM10000)
L00093	dataset 15 .
L00094	HAM10000 contains 10,015 dermoscopic images of pigmented skin lesions collected from patients at two study sites in Australia and Austria, with their diagnoses confirmed by either histopathology, confocal microscopy, clinical follow-up visits, or expert consensus.
L00095	The 7 disease labels in the
L00096	dataset cover 95% of the lesions encountered in clinical practice 15 .
L00097	Because of these meritorious properties, HAM10000 is a good candidate
L00098	dataset for dermatological analysis, as aimed for with DermaMNIST.
L00099	While the “ lightweight ” nature of DermaMNIST due to its “ small size ” is appealing for its adoption in machine learning for biomedical imaging 39 , the low spatial resolution (28 × 28) does not capture sufficient morphological structures of skin lesions compared
L00100	to the source
L00101	dataset HAM10000.
L00102	Despite this, DermaMNIST has been used for a wide variety of applications in peer-reviewed publications: semi- and self-supervised learning 41 – 43 , federated learning 44 – 46 , privacy-preserving learning 47 , 48 , neural architecture search 49 , 50 , adversarially robust learning
L00103	51 , 52 , data augmentation 53 , 54 , generative modeling 55 , model interpretability 56 , AutoML 57 , active learning 58 , quantum vision transformers 59 , and biomedical vision-language foundation models 60 – 62 , as well as derivative benchmark
L00104	datasets 63 , 64 .
L00105	However, as we investigate below, the resulting DermaMNIST and its benchmarks suffer from serious flaws.
L00106	Data Leakage A caveat of HAM10000, despite its rather large size, is that it contains multiple images of the same lesion captured either from different viewing angles or at different magnification levels (Fig.
L00107	1(a) ), i.e., the number of lesions with unique lesion IDs (HAM_xxx) is smaller than the number of images with unique image IDs (ISIC_xxx).
L00108	We visualize the frequency counts of lesions and how many images of the same lesion are present in HAM10000 in Fig.
L00109	1(b) and observe that the 10,015 images are in fact derived from only 7,470 unique lesions, and 1,956 of these lesion IDs (~26.18%) contains 2 or more images: 1,423 lesions have 2 images, 490 lesions have 3 images, 34 lesions have 4 images, 5 lesions
L00110	have 5 images, and 6 lesions have 4 images each.
L00111	Unfortunately, this was not accounted for when preparing train-valid-test splits for DermaMNIST.
L00112	The DermaMNIST
L00113	dataset is released as a pre-processed NumPy array, so while the
L00114	dataset does not contain filenames of the images, we confirmed this by contacting the authors to obtain the exact training-validation-testing split filenames.
L00115	Therefore, there is considerable data leakage of images of the same lesion across partitions.
L00116	Fig.
L00117	1(a) shows 2 examples where images of the same lesion are present in the training, validation, and testing partitions.
L00118	This issue is quite pervasive across DermaMNIST, and our analysis found the following overlaps across partitions: train-test: 886 images (641 lesions), train-valid: 440 images (332 lesions); valid-test: 128 images (113 lesions); train-valid-test: 51 images (40 lesions) (Fig.
L00119	1(c) ).
L00120	Such data leakage naturally raises concerns on the reliability of the DermaMNIST benchmarks and related studies.
L00121	Fig.
L00122	1 DermaMNIST analyses: (a,b) show instances of and reasons for the data leakage, and (c) visualizes how the three
L00123	datasets: DermaMNIST, DermaMNIST-C, and DermaMNIST-E differ in their partition composition, yet have similarly proportionate diagnosis distributions.
L00124	Images from DermaMNIST are licensed under CC BY-NC 4.0 38 , 39 .
L00125	Best viewed online.
L00126	We correct this data leakage by simply moving all images of a lesion ID present in the train partition from valid and test partitions back to the train partition.
L00127	We choose to do this (i.e., moving the images of a lesion to the training set) instead of removing the images completely, to ensure that images of the same lesion are in one partition while also not discarding any images.
L00128	Although this has the undesirable side effect of increasing the training partition size at the cost of reduced validation and testing partition sizes, it fixes the data leakage issue by ensuring there is no overlap across partitions.
L00129	Fig.
L00130	2 Visualizing how DermaMNIST’s incorrect resizing operation leads to loss of information.
L00131	DermaMNIST’s approach (top row) to generating 224 × 224 images
L00132	results in visibly pixelated images.
L00133	Our approach (bottom row), used for both DermaMNIST-C and DermaMNIST-E, retains much more detailed information.

L00220	Results on 224 × 224 resolution DermaMNIST is created by resizing images from HAM10000’s original 600 × 450 spatial resolution to MNIST-like 28 × 28 resolution using (bi)cubic spline interpolation.
L00221	However, for their classification benchmark experiments on the 224 × 224 resolution, instead of resizing the original images to 224 × 224, the authors 38 , 39 upsample the low-resolution 28 × 28 to obtain the 224 × 224 using nearest neighbor interpolation (“ 224
L00222	(resized from 28) ”; verifiable through their source code 70 ).
L00223	This, unsurprisingly, leads to quite blurry images, given the unrecoverable information lost when downsampling from the original 600 × 450 to 28 × 28 and leads to significant loss of detail (e.g., dermoscopic structures and artifacts) in the images used to train models on 224
L00224	× 224 images (Fig.
L00225	2 ).
L00226	Our approach of directly downsampling from the original high-resolution to 224 × 224 to create DermaMNIST-C and DermaMNIST-E, used when reporting
L00227	results in Table 1 , yields conspicuously more detailed outputs (Fig.
L00228	2 ).
L00229	Extending DermaMNIST Finally, although DermaMNIST-C is a good light-weight
L00230	dataset of choice for evaluating machine learning models on dermatological tasks and for educational purposes, as MedMNIST intended to be, the quantitative
L00231	results of DermaMNIST-C (and DermaMNIST for that matter) perhaps paint a deceptively optimistic picture of the state of automated dermatological diagnosis models.
L00232	We propose a more challenging extension of DermaMNIST named DermaMNIST-E .
L00233	The original DermaMNIST and its corrected version DermaMNIST-C are based on HAM10000, which was used as the training partition for the ISIC Challenge 2018.
L00234	However, the ISIC Challenge 2018 had, apart from the 10,015 training images from HAM10000, separate validation and testing partitions containing 193 and 1,512 images, respectively.
L00235	Therefore, we create DermaMNIST-E with DermaMNIST as training set and ISIC 2018 validation and test partitions as validation and testing sets, respectively.
L00236	Although the official testing partition of ISIC 2018 contained 1,512 images, we remove one image known as the “easter egg” (ISIC_0035068) 68 , resulting in a total of 1,511 images.
L00237	While the resulting diagnosis distribution across partitions for DermaMNIST-E is similar to that of DermaMNIST-C and DermaMNIST (Fig.
L00238	1(c) ), our benchmark
L00239	results on DermaMNIST-E (Table 1 ) show that it is indeed a more challenging
L00240	dataset and is guaranteed to be void of any data leakage.
L00241	It should be noted that the DermaMNIST-E
L00242	dataset is almost the same as the official partitions of the ISIC 2018 Challenge data with 2 distinctions: the images in DermaMNIST-E are resized (28 × 28 or 224 × 224) and the “easter egg” image has been removed from the testing partition.
L00243	A summary of the three
L00244	datasets: DermaMNIST, DermaMNIST-C, and DermaMNIST-E is presented in Table 4 , listing a brief description and statistics of the
L00245	datasets.
L00246	Fitzpatrick17k Released in 2021, Fitzpatrick17k is one of the largest publicly available
L00247	datasets of clinical skin disease images.
L00248	The large number of skin diseases covered (114), the in-the-wild nature of the images therein, and the availability of associated and diverse Fitzpatrick skin tone (FST) labels 71 make it an immensely valuable
L00249	dataset for skin image analysis research.
L00250	However, unlike DermaMNIST, which is collected from clinical visits and whose labels were confirmed, Fitzpatrick17k was curated from 2 publicly available online dermatological atlases: DermaAmin 72 (12,672 images) and Atlas Dermatologico 73 (3,905 images).
L00251	As such, the diagnosis labels of these images are not confirmed, through histopathology or otherwise.
L00252	The authors conducted a small-scale study on only 3.04% of the entire
L00253	dataset (504 out of 16,577 images) where 2 board-certified dermatologists assessed the diagnoses of the images, and the consensus was that only 69.0% of the images were clearly diagnostic of the disease label and, more importantly, 3.4% of the images were mislabeled.
L00254	This is problematic since Fitzpatrick17k has been used to train models for high-stakes applications such as model explainability 74 , 75 , trustworthiness 76 , skin tone detection 77 , model calibration 78 and fairness 79 – 84 .

L00323	Erroneous images In a recent study, Pakzad et al .
L00324	80 reported the presence of erroneous or outlier non-skin images in Fitzpatrick17k.
L00325	Using an outlier detection approach based on distance to the nearest neighbors in the embedding space, we rank images in the
L00326	dataset based on their probability of being an outlier.
L00327	Sample outliers include non-dermatological imaging modalities (e.g., histopathology, radiology, microscopy, fundus), images of plants (leaves, trees) and animals (e.g., rodents, bugs, poultry), etc.
L00328	Worryingly, Fitzpatrick17k does not contain information regarding which images are non-dermatological, which consequently impacts the training and evaluation of models.
L00329	Non-standardized data partitioning The Fitzpatrick17k benchmarks by Groh et al .
L00330	20 , as well as several works that followed 75 , 79 , 82 , 84 , also suffer from another major problem: the lack of a strictly held-out test partition.
L00331	For all their skin condition prediction experiments, the authors only used a training and a validation set, and used the terms “validation” and “testing” interchangeably in the paper.
L00332	This can also be verified in their accompanying code implementation, where the data partitions used to select the best epoch during training 102 (“ the epoch with the lowest loss on the validation set ”) and to report the final
L00333	results 103 are the same.
L00334	This violates the fundamental rules of machine learning model training and evaluation, where the validation and the testing partitions must be separate disjoint sets, and the former is used for choosing the best performing model during training and hyperparameter selection, while the latter is reserved
L00335	only for the final model evaluation and is never used during training.
L00336	Correcting Fitzpatrick17k In light of the numerous aforementioned issues with Fitzpatrick17k: data duplication, conflicting labels, the presence of erroneous images, and the absence of a well-defined test partition, we attempt to clean up Fitzpatrick17k and present a smaller, yet more reliable
L00337	dataset.
L00338	Specifically, we remove clusters of duplicates (this includes duplicate pairs), keeping one image from each cluster if there are no conflicting diagnosis or FST labels within the cluster, i.e., a “homogenous cluster”.
L00339	Next, we remove the erroneous images from the
L00340	dataset and refer to this “cleaned” version of Fitzpatrick17k as Fitzpatrick17k-C.
L00341	In the absence of standardized
L00342	dataset partitions, researchers who used Fitzpatrick17k for their models had to resort to generating their own splits 74 , 79 , 80 , making it very hard for models across papers to be compared.
L00343	To resolve this, we present standardized training, validation, and testing partitions for Fitzpatrick17k-C for the skin image analysis community to use, obtained by splitting Fitzpatrick17k-C in the ratio of 70:10:20, stratified on the diagnosis labels.

L00403	DermaMNIST Data leakage and benchmarks The extent of data leakage in DermaMNIST emanating from improper data partitioning is quite severe, with 1,006 of the 7,470 unique lesions (~13.47%) in the
L00404	dataset being present in more than 1 partition (Fig.
L00405	1(c) ).
L00406	Patient-level stratification, or in this case, lesion-level stratification, is crucial to ensure that the model does not “see” the lesions in the held-out test set while training.
L00407	Consequently, the lack of such a stratification implies that the corresponding benchmark classification
L00408	results are not truly reflective of the models’ generalization capability and, therefore, we strongly suspect that the models’ performance could be inflated.
L00409	On the other hand, since most works use the entire HAM10000
L00410	dataset for training instead of splitting it like in the case of DermaMNIST, undetected duplicates in HAM10000 are arguably a less severe issue.
L00411	However, a word of caution on comparing the benchmark
L00412	results of DermaMNIST and DermaMNIST-C (Table 1 ): the two
L00413	datasets do not share the same test partitions.
L00414	Specifically, DermaMNIST-C’s test set was obtained from DermaMNIST’s test set by removing all images whose lesion IDs were present in the test set, and therefore, the former is a subset of the other (i.e., DermaMNIST-C test set ⊂ DermaMNIST test set).
L00415	Similarly, as shown in Fig.
L00416	1(c) , the training set of DermaMNIST-C is larger than that of DermaMNIST.
L00417	For these reasons, contrary to the assumption that fixing data leakage in DermaMNIST should decrease performance benchmarks, we emphasize that performance benchmarks for DermaMNIST and DermaMNIST-C should not be compared since these models have been trained and evaluated on dissimilar partitions.
L00418	A more challenging
L00419	dataset While HAM10000 is indeed a valuable
L00420	dataset for skin image analysis research, models trained on HAM10000 do not necessarily perform well when evaluated on other skin lesion image
L00421	datasets 104 .
L00422	This was also observed by the organizers of the ISIC 2018 Challenge 65 where they relied on external testing data for ranking the submissions (" multipartition test sets containing data not reflected in training
L00423	dataset are an effective way to differentiate the ability of algorithms to generalize ”).
L00424	Therefore, following the ISIC 2018 Challenge, we use the images from the Challenge’s validation and testing partitions, resized to 28 × 28 and 224 × 224, to create the validation and testing partitions, respectively, of the newly proposed DermaMNIST-E
L00425	dataset, thus allowing for a more robust assessment of the skin lesion diagnosis models trained on this
L00426	dataset.
L00427	Incorrect scaling of images Another issue with the DermaMNIST benchmarks was the use of 28 × 28 images to report the
L00428	results on 224 × 224 resolutions.
L00429	As we show in Fig.
L00430	2 , the information lost when downsampling from 600 × 450 to 28 × 28 is quite significant, and it is impossible to recover this when upsampling from 28 × 28 to 224 × 224.
L00431	This can also be observed in the quantitative
L00432	results (Table 1 ): intuitively, given that we ensure that the models do not overfit, we would expect that a model with larger capacity (ResNet-50) trained on high- resolution images (224 × 224) should perform better than a lower capacity model (ResNet-18) trained on lower
L00433	resolution images (28 × 28).
L00434	However, this is not the case with DermaMNIST
L00435	results, where ResNet-18/28 × 28 models perform [AUC; ACC] better than ResNet-50/224 × 224 models: [0.917; 0.735] versus [0.912; 0.731].
L00436	On the other hand, with DermaMNIST-C and DermaMNIST-E, ResNet-50/224 × 224 models do perform better than their ResNet-18/28 × 28 counterparts.

L00447	results.
L00448	Our
L00449	dataset cleaning pipeline, publicly available on GitHub 105 , is highly modular and configurable, allowing users to adjust similarity thresholds to exclude and use multiple duplicate lists (fastdup and cleanvision) for cleaning, decide whether they want to remove duplicate clusters altogether or retain one representative
L00450	image from each cluster, decide whether they want to remove images with unknown FST labels, and decide which outliers to exclude based on a similarity threshold.
L00451	While we can claim with a high degree of certainty that the new cleaned
L00452	dataset Fitzpatrick17k-C will be devoid of any duplicates, the large scale of manual review required makes it nearly impossible to guarantee it.
L00453	Dealing with conflicting diagnosis and FST labels Several duplicates in Fitzpatrick17k, despite being near identical copies, do not share the same diagnosis labels.
L00454	This affects the training and evaluation of models, since a model can be incorrectly penalized for its prediction because of the conflicting labels.
L00455	However, correcting these labels, so that copies of the same image have the same diagnosis label, requires a domain expert (i.e., a dermatologist) to go through all the images and confirm and correct their labels.
L00456	Unfortunately, even if such an endeavor were to be undertaken, these diagnoses will not be histopathology-confirmed and the accuracy of diagnoses confirmed through images alone (a scenario similar to “store-and-forward” teledermatology) is expected to be lower than those confirmed through in-person patient visits 106 –
L00457	108 .
L00458	Another approach to resolving the diagnosis label conflicts could be through mapping the diagnoses in Fitzpatrick17k to the World Health Organization (WHO) International Classification of Diseases, Eleventh Revision (ICD-11) 109 , and if multiple diagnoses belong to the same “parent”, their label conflict can be
L00459	resolved by assigning the “parent”’s label to both.
L00460	However, when mapping the diagnoses in Fitzpatrick17k to using the ICD-11 Browser 110 we ran into the following issues: Several diagnosis labels did not yield any matches (e.g., “acquired autoimmune bullous diseaseherpes gestationis”, “nematode infection”, “neurotic excoriations”, “pediculosis lids”).
L00461	There were diagnosis labels where one of the labels has an entry in ICD-11, but other seemingly related labels do not (e.g., “basal cell carcinoma” exists in ICD-11, but possibly related “basal cell carcinoma morpheiform” and “solid cystic basal cell carcinoma” do not).
L00462	There were labels where we found near but not exact matches (e.g., the label “erythema annulare centrifigum” in Fitzpatrick17k does not have an exact match, but ICD-11 contains “Erythema annulare”; other partial matching [Fitzpatrick17k; ICD-11] labels include ["hidradenitis”; “Hidradenitis suppurativa”], ["fixed eruptions”, “Fixed drug eruption”],
L00463	["lupus subacute”, “Subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus”], ["porokeratosis actinic”, “Disseminated superficial porokeratosis actinic”], etc.
L00464	Overall, 8 diagnosis labels had no matches in ICD-11.
L00465	An additional 29 diagnoses yielded partial matches to entries in ICD-11, and therefore could not be reliably mapped to a single entry.
L00466	For these diagnoses, i.e., those that had partial or no matches in ICD-11, we expanded our search to the ICD-11 Coding Tool Mortality and Morbidity Statistics (MMS) 111 and the ICD-11 Classification of Dermatological Diseases 112 , 113 , but this did not resolve any
L00467	issues.
L00468	Additionally, we also carried out a similar diagnosis lookup on the Systematized Medical Nomenclature of Medicine - Clinical Terminology (SNOMED-CT), a comprehensive clinical global healthcare terminology that may better represent medical vocabulary than ICD-11 114 .
L00469	Although we were able to find more matches to SNOMED-CT Identifiers 115 (SCTIDs) than to entries in ICD-11 and its derivatives, it was still not perfect: we ended up with 17 and 3 diagnosis labels with partial and no matches, respectively.
L00470	We make the
L00471	results of our Fitzpatrick17k diagnosis-to-{ICD-11 classification code, SCTID} mapping publicly available online.
L00472	As a recommendation for the future
L00473	datasets containing clinical images of skin diseases, we believe that adding either ICD classification codes or SNOMED-CT Identifiers (SCTIDs) for the disease labels would be a helpful addition to the metadata, and would greatly enhance the usability of such
L00474	datasets for hierarchical diagnosis
L00475	methods 7 – 9 .
L00476	Similar conflicts were observed for FST labels of images, where images with very high similarity varied in their FST labels, sometimes by as much as 4 tones (Fig.
L00477	9 ).
L00478	A potential solution to this would be to re-assess the images with conflicting FST labels.
L00479	This would involve either obtaining manual and verified healthy skin segmentation masks or using an automated healthy skin segmentation method, followed by mapping the skin tone estimated using these healthy skin pixels to an FST label.
L00480	However, as noted by Groh et al .
L00481	20 , collecting manual segmentation masks is expensive, and automated skin segmentation approaches suffer from their own set of challenges 116 , 117 , including but not limited to susceptibility to non-standardized illumination, the presence of multiple objects, and/or low quality images (we direct the
L00482	readers to our arXiv pre-print 100 which contains several more visualizations which could not make it to the publisher’s version of the paper).
L00483	The presence of diffuse pathologies such as “acne”, “acne vulgaris”, and “disseminated actinic porokeratosis”, where the diseased regions of the skin are spread over a wide region and the exact boundaries between diseased and healthy regions are not well-defined, further compounds the task for any
L00484	automated healthy skin detection approach.
L00485	Because of these intractable issues, we make the decision of removing duplicate images that have conflicting diagnosis or FST labels.
L00486	While we realize that relabeling might be able to solve these label conflicts, we also acknowledge the enormous time, effort, and expenses such an endeavor would entail.
L00487	Updated benchmarks As discussed above, Groh et al .
L00488	20 used non-standardized
L00489	dataset partitions for training and evaluating their models for benchmarking, the most notable issue being the absence of a well-defined and held-out test set, thus violating one of the most basic rules of machine learning.
L00490	The use of the same
L00491	dataset partition for validation, i.e., choosing the best performing models, and for testing ensures that the reported performance is the best possible, but it is a poor reflection of the models’ generalization capabilities, since the models have been overfit to the testing data.
L00492	This issue, coupled with the presence of duplicates in Fitzpatrick17k, which would inevitably lead to data leakage across partitions, is the reason for the difference between Fitzpatrick17k model benchmarks by Groh et al . and our Fitzpatrick17k-C benchmarks (Table 2 ).
L00493	For the “Verified” experiments (i.e., “ testing on the subset of images labeled by a board-certified dermatologist as diagnostic of the labeled condition and training on the rest of the data ” 20 ), the number of training images for Fitzpatrick17k decreased from 16,229 to
L00494	10,060, meaning the models were trained on considerably fewer images.
L00495	On the other hand, the number of testing images in Fitzpatrick17k-C decreased by ~ 38% (from 348 in Fitzpatrick17k to 215) after
L00496	dataset cleaning, implying that 133 images were duplicates in Fitzpatrick17k’s testing partition, which explains why their benchmark
L00497	results were inflated.
L00498	Similar trends can be observed across all the benchmark experiments: the number of training images for “Source A”, “Source B”, and “Fitz 1-2 & 5-6” experiments nearly halved after
L00499	dataset cleaning, and the number of both training and testing images for the “Fitz 1-4” experiments decreased by more than 50%.
L00500	We believe that the newly proposed Fitzpatrick17k-C along with the well-defined and disjoint training, validation, and testing partitions, the benchmarks, and the publicly available training and evaluation code will help other researchers better utilize the
L00501	dataset and make comparison across
L00502	methods easier and standardized.
L00503	Comparison to other works One of the first works on analyzing skin image analysis
L00504	datasets by Abhishek 34 focused on ISIC Challenge
L00505	datasets for the skin lesion segmentation task, specifically on the challenges from 2016, 2017, and 2018.
L00506	They found these
L00507	datasets to have considerable overlap across their training partitions, and this can be attributed to all of them being subsets of the ISIC Archive 118 .
L00508	Their analysis, however, was limited to overlap detection simply based on the filenames, since all the filenames in the ISIC Archive follow the template ISIC_{ISIC identifier}.jpg.
L00509	Specific to Fitzpatrick17k, Pakzad et al.’s work 80 was the first to mention the presence of “erroneous and wrongly labeled images”, and how the images therein have non-standard illumination and camera perspectives, highlighting the need for cleaning Fitzpatrick17k.
L00510	Groger et al .
L00511	37 conducted a data quality analysis on the presence of “irrelevant samples”, near duplicates, and label errors in 6
L00512	datasets.
L00513	They used a self-supervised DL-based method for generating rankings of images potentially containing data quality issues, following which, 3 experts including a board-certified dermatologist manually reviewed the images and answered a questionnaire.
L00514	The authors also noted that non-expert annotations may be sufficient for confirming duplicate images.
L00515	Vega et al .
L00516	36 analyzed a popular and well-cited monkeypox skin image
L00517	dataset, and found it to contain “medically irrelevant images”.
L00518	They discovered that the images were extracted from online repositories through web-scraping and lacked medical validation.
L00519	Finally, their experiments showed that the claims made by the
L00520	dataset’s authors about the utility of the images might not have been true, since a model trained by Vega et al . on “blinded” images (i.e., images where the regions of interest related to the disease were covered by black rectangles) was also able to
L00521	accurately classify the diseases.
L00522	The closest
L00523	dataset analysis to our work, both in the scale of the analyses and the sizes of the
L00524	datasets analyzed, is the work by Cassidy et al .
L00525	35 on the ISIC Challenge
L00526	datasets from 2016 through 2020, where the authors employed a multi-step duplicate removal strategy.
L00527	First, similar to our previous work 34 , they removed duplicates across
L00528	dataset partitions based on filenames.
L00529	Next, they used the following tools and
L00530	methods on “ a random selection of training images for 72 hours ”: (a) a Python library called ImageHash to measure similarity based on several hashing
L00531	methods (average, perceptual, difference, and wavelet), (b) mean-squared error (MSE)-based image similarity detection, (c) structural similarity index measure (SSIM)-based image similarity detection, and (d) cosine similarity-based detection.
L00532	Finally, they used a Python library called FSlint to detect duplicates based on MD5 and SHA-1 file checksum signatures.
L00533	The analyses of the three
L00534	datasets in this paper: DermaMNIST, HAM10000, and Fitzpatrick17k arguably goes beyond those presented in these previous works.
L00535	For DermaMNIST, since we were able to request the exact training-validation-testing splits’ filenames, we could simply cross-reference those to the publicly available HAM10000 metadata to detect and correct data leakage.
L00536	Our analysis of Fitzpatrick17k is much more in-depth and involved: we start with finding similar images and erroneous images in the embeddings’ latent space, followed by a manual non-expert review to confirm the duplicates, and then coalesce duplicates to form larger clusters wherever applicable.
L00537	The filenames in Fitzpatrick17k follow the template <MD5_hash>.jpg, meaning that all the images have a unique MD5 checksum, which in turn implies that file checksum-based duplicate detection would not be possible.
L00538	The Python library cleanvision that we use for duplicate detection already relies upon ImageHash for hashing-based similarity measurement.
L00539	Measuring similarity scores in the embedding space is arguably superior to doing so in the input space (e.g., MSE, SSIM-based duplication checks by Cassidy et al .
L00540	35 ), since a latent representation goes beyond pixel-level information and is able to better capture semantic information about the image as a whole.
L00541	Finally, because of the large scale of Fitzpatrick17k (16,577 images), manual review by experts become cost prohibitive.
L00542	Data leakage and reproducibility crisis Kapoor et al .
L00543	25 presented an in-depth review of instances of data leakage in machine learning (ML) applications across 17 scientific domains, and how such leakage can lead to a reproducibility crisis in ML-based science.
L00544	They also proposed a hierarchical taxonomy of leakage types, which has 8 types of leakage across 2 levels.
L00545	Our analysis of DermaMNIST shows that it suffers from “ [L1.4] : Duplicates in
L00546	datasets ” and “ [L3.2] : Non-independence between train and test samples ”, where the latter is a direct consequence of the former.
L00547	Concerningly, Fitzpatrick17k exhibited three types of data leakage: “ [L1.1] : No test set ”, since it did not have a disjoint held-out test set and had duplicate images, along with the aforementioned [L1.4] and [L3.2] .
L00548	In
L00549	conclusion, in this paper, we examined the data quality of three popular skin image analysis
L00550	datasets: DermaMNIST from the MedMNIST collection, its source HAM10000, and Fitzpatrick17k.
L00551	For DermaMNIST, we investigated the extent of data leakage across its partitions and proposed two new and improved versions: a corrected
L00552	dataset that does not have any leakage (DermaMNIST-C) and an extended and arguably more challenging
L00553	dataset (DermaMNIST-E) that is almost the same as the ISIC 2018 Challenge
L00554	dataset except the images are resized to 28 × 28 and 224 × 224 and the “easter egg” image is removed from the test partition.
L00555	We also investigated the presence of duplicates in HAM10000, the source
L00556	dataset for DermaMNIST, and discovered 18 new duplicate image pairs that were unaccounted for in the metadata.
L00557	For Fitzpatrick17k, we conducted a systematic analysis encompassing data duplication, mislabeling of diagnosis and skin tone labels, as well as the identification of outlier images, followed by cleaning the
L00558	dataset to propose a cleaned version Fitzpatrick17k-Cwith standardized training, validation, and testing partitions.
L00559	We also showed how Fitzpatrick17k contains labels that do not fully align, which we recommend they should, with internationally recognized standards such as ICD-11 and SNOMED-CT, limiting its utility for hierarchical and differential diagnosis-based approaches.
L00560	For all the
L00561	datasets, we conducted benchmark evaluations using multiple
L00562	methods, repeated 3 times for robustness.
L00563	The primary
L00564	objective of this paper is to raise awareness about potential data quality issues that may arise in large
L00565	datasets, and how these issues can go unnoticed even in popular
L00566	datasets, casting doubts on the
L00567	conclusions made about the robustness and the generalizability of the models trained on these
L00568	datasets.
L00569	We hope this can serve as a call to action for more stringent data quality assessments.
L00570	To facilitate this, we plan to make our evaluation method and code publicly available so that they can be used and extended upon for evaluating more existing and new
L00571	datasets.
L00572	Methods DermaMNIST Detecting and correcting data leakage We used HAM10000’s publicly available metadata, which contains image ID to lesion ID mappings, and DermaMNIST partitions’ filenames, which contain image IDs, to perform an inner-join using the Python library pandas.

L00574	To correct the leakage, we follow this approach: if images of a lesion ID exist in the training partition, move all images belonging to that lesion ID from validation and testing partitions to the training.

L00628	Correcting Fitzpatrick17k The Fitzpatrick17k cleaning pipeline consists of the following steps: Similarity score-based filtering: We specify an image similarity threshold of 0.99, meaning any image whose maximum similarity score to any other image in the

L00643	of the above hyperparameter settings with different seed values, effectively training 2 × 3 × 4 × 7 × 3 = 504 models.
L00644	For each experimental setting, the hyperparameter setting with the highest accuracy on the validation partition was used for the final testing, and the
L00645	results are reported in Table 2 .
L00646	Finally, we used mixed precision training through Hugging Face Accelerate 124 to speed up the training.
L00647	For all the experiments, we use the same image transformations as those in Groh et al.’s work.
L00648	The reported metrics for evaluation were the overall and the FST-wise classification accuracies.
L00649	All models were trained and evaluated using PyTorch 121 and Accelerate 124 .
L00650	To understand how sensitive these classification models are to the hyperparameter choices, we also evaluated models optimized on one experiment’s best hyperparameters on another experiment’s test set, and these
L00651	results are presented in Table 3 .
L00652	The columns represent the optimal hyperparameters for each setting, and the rows represent the overall test set accuracies for all the settings when evaluated using those particular hyperparameters.
L00653	We observe that varying the hyperparameters does not considerably affect the test accuracies.
